Machu Picchu

A 15th-century Inca citadel!

"Machu Pikchu"

In the Quechua language, machu means "old" or "old person", while pikchu means either
"portion of coca being chewed" or "pyramid, pointed multi-sided solid; cone".
Thus the name of the site is sometimes interpreted as "old mountain".

Quite the hike!

There once was a time when the only way to get to Machu Picchu was by hiking the "Inca Trail".
The world famous trek is 26 miles from start to finish and takes 4 days to complete.
Now tourists can catch a short bus ride up the the site from the nearby town, Aguas Calientes.

Llamas of Machu Picchu

The llamas are perhaps the most beloved mammals of the Andes; The llamas live in the mountain regions of Chile, Argentina, Bolivia and Peru,
where they contributed greatly in the development of the vast Inca Empire.
Nowadays they captivate the curiosity of the people who visit the Inca City of Machu Picchu.

What a view!

The ancient site is located in the Eastern Cordillera of southern Peru, on a 7,970 ft mountain ridge.
This makes for some truly breathtaking panoramas.

Inca Masonry

Machu Picchu was built around 1450 AD using simple tools. Roughly 200 structures survived throughout the ruins.
Fortunatley the decay stopped at the perfectly cut granite stones,
which remain impeccable and showcase the unbelievable skill of the Inca masons.

Astronomy Mirrors

Located below the Sun Temple in Machu Picchu are two shallow basins used by the Inca to observe the stars.
Just like the Mayans, the Incas were keen observers of the sky.
They filled the basisns with water to mirror and observe the images of the stars.

Huayna Picchu

Wayna Pikchu, in Quechua, is a mountain in Peru which the Urubamba River bends.
It is located in the Cusco Region, Urubamba Province, Machupicchu District.
It rises over Machu Picchu, the so-called lost city of the Incas.
The Incas built a trail up the side of the Huayna Picchu and built temples and terraces on its top.
The peak of Huayna Picchu is 8,835 ft above sea level, or about 850 ft higher than Machu Picchu.
